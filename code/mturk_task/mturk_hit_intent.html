What is the true intent?
Goal of study:

In this study, we are analyzing the questioning of witnesses in U.S. congressional hearings. We are interested in understanding how audiences (you) perceive intent in argumentative conversations, and how that perception changes depending on the personal beliefs of different audiences.

About Congressional hearings:

A Congressional hearing is called by Congress and serves as a forum to get facts and opinions from a witness. A witness is usually a politician, an academic, or a private corporate official. Hearings are held to investigate suspicions of wrongdoing or to review the quality of federal programs and performance of government officials.

Your task:

You are presented with question-response pairs from a congressional hearing.

For each question, your task is to judge the true intent of the questioner
  • Is the politican attacking or favoring the witness?
For each response, your task is to judge the true intent of the witness
  • Is the witness answering or evading the question?
Your Task: Detailed Instructions and Examples (Click to hide/show)

In this task, you are presented with a series of question-response pairs from a U.S. congressional hearing (in chronological order).

The questions are asked by a politician, a member of Congress. The responses to the questions are given by a witness.

People usually speak one at a time, and take turns speaking. Each turn in the conversation starts with the name of the speaker and their title.

  1. Questioner Intent: How does the politician pose their question(s)?
    • includes leading questions, controversial assumptions, or attacks:
      • Chairman Hensarling: Isn't it true, Mr. Stumpf, that in the settlement, no one admits guilt? (you believe the politician is leading the witness to an unfavorable 'yes' answer)
      • Mr. Wilson: What changes need to be made to have greater success in countering Hezbollah? (you believe the politician makes the controversial assumption that little or no success has been made in fighting Hezbollah)
      • More examples:
      • Mr. Deutch: Do you think it would be reasonable for the members of this committee to conclude that the President by first interfering in one investigation and then interfering in an investigation into himself committed obstruction of justice? (you believe the politician includes the controversial assumption that Trump interfered in investigations)
    • includes questions or statements favoring the witness:
      • Mr. Guthrie (to Mark Zuckerberg, CEO of Facebook): I had a lot of people complain about ads but it is something I am willing to accept in return for a free and valuable service. So you are not unique in Silicon Valley in doing this type of targeted ads, are you? (you believe the politician is favoring Zuckerberg and asking him an easy question that makes Facebook look good)
  2. Witness Intent

    3. Use your personal beliefs and experiences to make this judgment. Multiple choices may fit, but choose the one that fits best.

    1. How does the witness respond to the question(s)?
      • answers all reasonable questions
        • Description:
          • You believe the witness answers all reasonable questions in a straightforward manner. In the case of being asked multiple questions, you can decide whether it is reasonable to expect the witness to answer all or only some of the questions.
          • Q: Mr. Cohen, who said that?
            Mr. Cohen: I did. (you believe Cohen directly answers the question)
          • Q: One of many, but not the sole factor?
            Mr. Powell: No. It is simply one of many. (you believe Powell directly answers the question)
          • Q: Can you elaborate on that?
            Mr. Powell: So, before the financial crisis... (you believe Powell indirectly answers the question)
      • answers partially or not all questions
        • Description:
          • You believe the witness provides a partial answer because you believe they either gave an incomplete answer or answered only some of the questions being asked.
          You may also assume the politician asks an implied question; that is, a question not explicitly asked but obvious from the context. For example, the question "Can you elaborate on that?" naturally implies the question "What other information do you have on that?". Responding with only "Yes" answers the literal question but doesn't answer the implied question.
        • Possible Intents:
          • You believe the witness has one of these intents:
          • to dodge an unfavorable question or question part
            • Q: Do you, Mr. Cohen, recognize the gravity of your offenses? You are a lawyer, right?
              Mr. Cohen: As of yesterday, I am no longer a lawyer. I have lost my law license, amongst other things. (you believe Cohen doesn't answer the first question of whether he recognizes the gravity of his offenses)
            • Q: Would Don Jr. or Mr. Weisselberg have more information about that?
              Mr. Cohen: Mr. Weisselberg for sure about the entire discussions, and Don Jr. would have cursory information. (you believe Cohen doesn't answer the implied question "What is the other information?", and instead only answers the literal question of whether or not they have information.)
          • for other justified reason
            • Q: What are you looking for in the labor market? How much slack do we have left?
              Mr. Powell: We look at a very broad range of indicators [...] And you also asked--? (you believe Powell genuinely forgot the second question)
            • Q: Do you, Mr. Cohen, recognize the gravity of your offenses? You are a lawyer, right?
              Mr. Cohen: As of yesterday, I am no longer a lawyer. I have lost my law license, amongst other things. (you believe the first question is rhetorical so Cohen doesn't need to answer it)
      • answers a different question
        • Description:
          • You believe the witness doesn't answer the original question but instead shifts the question so they can answer a different question.
        • Possible Intents:
          • You believe the witness has one of these intents:
          • to reformulate or evade an unfavorable question
            • Q: But if you saw the problem in one area of the business, why didn't you thoroughly investigate in the other areas?
              Mr. Stumpf: There is no question, Mr. Chairman, we should have done more sooner. (you believe Stumpf doesn't answer the tough question why he didn't investigate; he instead shifts the question to: Why didn't you respond better and sooner?)
            • Q: I understand that Facebook does not sell user data, per se, in the traditional sense. Why wasn't explaining what Facebook does with users' data higher priority for you as a cofounder and now as CEO?
              Mr. Zuckerberg: Mr. Chairman, you are right that we don't sell any data, and I would say that we do try to explain what we do as time goes on. It is a broad system. (you believe Zuckerberg doesn't answer the question of why explaining wasn't a higher priority; he instead shifts the question to: Do you sell user data? Do you explain this?)
          • to correct the questioner or other justified reason
            • Q: When her hard drive failed, if you were properly backing up all the information required under the Federal Records Act, you would've had all those emails in your backup, wouldn't you?
              Mr. Koskinen: All emails are not official records under any official records act. Only emails are saved that reflect agency actions. (you believe Koskinen doesn't answer the question "would you have the emails?" because he instead corrects the politician's assumption about what actually gets backed up.)
      • says they don't understand/don't know/can't answer or asks a clarification question
        • Description:
          • The witness doesn't answer any form of the question (original or shifted) and instead says they either don't understand the question (including asking clarification questions), don't know the answer or can't answer the question.
        • Possible Intents:
          • You believe the witness has one of these intents:
          • you believe they are lying (avoiding any answer, stalling, buying time)
            • Mr. Engel: Do you adjust your algorithms to prevent individuals interested in violence from being connected with other like-minded individuals?
              Mr. Zuckerberg: Sorry. Could you repeat that? (you believe Zuckerberg understood/heard the question, but stalls for time)
            • Mr. Meadows: Have you contacted anybody in the administration to advocate on your behalf?
              Mr. Cohen: I don't know what you are referring to, sir. (you believe Cohen knows, but stalls for time)
          • you believe they are sincere (they genuinely didn't understand or are not able to answer the question)
            • Q: Yes or no, will you make the commitment to change the user default settings?
              Mr. Zuckerbeg: Congressman, this is a complex issue that deserves more than a one-word answer. (you believe Zuckerberg can't answer because you agree that the response is not a simple yes/no answer)
            • Ms. Jackson Lee: Do you wish to change your testimony on June 13, where you said, ''I have never met with any Russians''?
              Attorney General Sessions: You're referring to my testimony at confirmation? (you believe Sessions is sincerely asking for clarification)

        • to sway opinion in their favor (for example, by painting the witness in a positive light, giving the witness the benefit of the doubt or a way out)

            Mr. Zuckerberg: Well, Mr. Chairman, I think we have evolved quite a bit as a company. When I started it, I certainly didn't think that we would be the ones building this broad of a community around the world. I thought someone would do it. I didn't think it was going to be us.             Mr. Powell: We don't believe it poses systemic kinds of risks, but we do think it poses a macroeconomic risk, particularly in the event of an economic downturn. And if there is a downturn, they will be less able to carry out their roles in the economy and that may have an amplification effect on a downturn. (you believe Powell adds helpful and critical details to understand his nuanced answer)
